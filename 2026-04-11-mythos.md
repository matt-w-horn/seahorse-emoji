### Security Is Becoming an Epidemiology Problem

*Matt Horn ([About](./README.md))*

*published April 11, 2026*

I've been thinking a lot about Claude Mythos and [Project Glasswing](https://www.anthropic.com/glasswing) this week. Anthropic announced a new AI model that is too dangerous to release publicly. This model can autonomously discover and exploit high-severity, multi-step vulnerability chains in nearly every major operating system, browser, and [other critical software](https://red.anthropic.com/2026/mythos-preview/), some of which have gone undetected for over 27 years. Over 50 organizations[^1] are now working to remediate these issues, and it's a massive headache for security engineers across the industry. [Jerome Powell and Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/10/powell-bessent-us-bank-ceos-anthropic-mythos-ai-cyber.html) this week met with the CEOs of most major banks to discuss the risks.

Mythos is dangerous, but it's also the solution. Released into the wild, adversaries would use it to compromise systems across computing infrastructure as rapidly and indiscriminately as COVID spread through the world in 2020 (or worse). Yet, applied with careful consideration in a controlled way, it serves as a form of inoculation against itself.

This analogy of immunity isn't entirely new. Most consumers already use the term "virus" to describe self-replicating malware that evades detection and persists on a host, and many strains are polymorphic, rewriting their own code on the fly. Many older machines that connected to the internet do not support modern encryption protocols, making HTTPS and TLS impossible. Zero-day vulnerabilities are found constantly in all systems, requiring a steady stream of security patches at every level of the software and firmware stack. Without these regular updates, a computing appliance is quick to find itself a victim of known exploits, many of which are publicly available and trivial to weaponize.

In the past, exploits were simple and the risk was limited. Many businesses, hospitals, and other organizations relied on systems involving paper and filing cabinets, and desktop computers were often novelties in many offices. Today, a serious breach can take down an entire nation's power grid or even launch weapons. A single vulnerability, once exploited, can take control of a commercial airliner in flight or a fleet of self-driving cars. The peaceful transfer of power in organizations and governments across the world often amounts to little more than the peaceful transfer of user credentials or nuclear launch codes. Nearly all significant financial transactions, both traditional banking and cryptocurrency, occur digitally. It's not hard to imagine a future where one nation steals all of the assets in another nation's treasury via hacking. Food systems, energy systems, financial systems, transportation systems: all of them rely on a backbone of highly imperfect, human-made computers.

This has led me to think about how AI and even traditional computing systems start to look more like parts of an early biological system. I don't mean in the sense of self-replication, but more in the sense of competition and adaptation pressure, like an advanced species newly encountering isolated protobacteria with limited defenses. Systems "exposed" to advanced AI in careful measure can likely develop some form of immunity, albeit a manual time-intensive effort facilitated by humans. But at the same time, AI (nothing other than a computer program) will likely continue to advance further while traditional software will require patches to keep up and maintain reasonable defenses.

Where will this go? What happens if a less responsible organization releases a system with similar capabilities into the wild, without consideration for the collateral damage? While these systems cannot yet directly harm us[^2] in the way a predator can, we are wildly reliant on computer systems for survival at scale in ways that we never have before. How will we handle this evolutionary arms race unfolding within computer systems? What will the long-term impacts be?

When I find these overlaps in a novel situation, I often consider what we can learn from biological sciences. I'm not an infectious disease expert, so I'd love to hear from those in the medical and life sciences communities.

For example, when vaccinating a population against an infectious disease, you typically don't need to vaccinate every single individual to eradicate the disease. Based on parameters of the disease, primarily its transmissibility (R₀), you can calculate that only a certain percentage of the population needs to be vaccinated before the disease is under control. This concept is called "[herd immunity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herd_immunity)".

As we continue to improve security using systems like Claude Mythos, effectively inoculating software libraries against threats posed by advanced AI systems, I've been curious whether concepts like herd immunity could help us more efficiently allocate resources. The entities aren't clearly defined the same way they are in animals, so I'm not sure how we'd measure it. But many of these exploits involve chaining multiple vulnerabilities together. Do we need to patch all of them? How do we know?

And certainly Mythos will not be the last model of this kind. Future models will have significantly more powerful cybersecurity capabilities. We're bound to discover even more deeply buried vulnerabilities. Perhaps future models even find flaws in the mathematics that underpins modern cryptography itself. And thus, like biological evolution, the competition between machine and machine will only accelerate. I'm not sure we're ready for that.

[^2]: I almost didn't include this line because many weapons are nearly or fully autonomous, and also Waymo exists, which could definitely become a lethal weapon.
